Bertrand Russell

The Philosopher, Logician, and Social Critic

Bertrand Russell's Life Timeline:

  • 1872: Bertrand Russell is born in Trellech, Wales, into an aristocratic family.
  • 1890: He enters Trinity College, Cambridge, where he studies mathematics and philosophy.
  • 1896: Publishes his first book, German Social Democracy.
  • 1903: Releases The Principles of Mathematics, establishing his reputation in logic and philosophy.
  • 1910-1913: Co-authors Principia Mathematica with Alfred North Whitehead, a seminal work in mathematical logic.
  • 1914: Publishes Our Knowledge of the External World, influencing analytic philosophy.
  • 1916: Dismissed from Trinity College for opposing World War I and imprisoned in 1918 for his anti-war activism.
  • 1920: Travels to Russia to observe the Bolshevik Revolution and later criticizes communism.
  • 1927: Publishes Why I Am Not a Christian, critiquing religious belief.
  • 1930: Writes The Conquest of Happiness, exploring the philosophy of personal well-being.
  • 1938: Moves to the United States and lectures at various universities.
  • 1940: Denied a teaching position at City College of New York due to his views on morality and religion.
  • 1943: Returns to Britain and focuses on writing and broadcasting.
  • 1948: Publishes Human Knowledge: Its Scope and Limits, examining epistemology.
  • 1950: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature for his humanitarian and philosophical writings.
  • 1955: Co-authors the Russell-Einstein Manifesto, warning against the dangers of nuclear war.
  • 1961: Imprisoned again for anti-nuclear protests.
  • 1967: Establishes the Bertrand Russell Peace Foundation.
  • 1970: Passes away in Penrhyndeudraeth, Wales, at the age of 97.
"Russell's clarity, incisiveness, and courage have set an example for generations of philosophers and intellectuals."

- Albert Einstein, Theoretical Physicist
